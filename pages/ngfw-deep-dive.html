4 Application Identification (App-ID)

What is App-ID?

App-ID identifies applications regardless of port, protocol, or encryption. This is the core NGFW differentiator.

Traditional Firewall:

"I see TCP port 443 traffic"

No idea if it's Office365 or malware C2

NGFW with App-ID:

"I see Microsoft Teams from user jsmith"

Full visibility into app, user, context

How App-ID Works (4-Stage Process)

1

Protocol Detection

Decodes protocol regardless of port. Detects "HTTP on port 8080".

2

Protocol Decoding

Decodes application layer. For HTTP: headers, URLs, methods.

3

Application Signatures

Matches against 3000+ app signatures. Updated from vendor cloud.

4

Heuristics

Behavioral patterns for unknown traffic.

Business Apps (Usually Allowed)

  • â€¢ office365-enterprise - M365
  • â€¢ ms-teams - Meetings

Risk Apps (Often Blocked)

  • â€¢ bittorrent - P2P

5 User Identification (User-ID)

What is User-ID?

User-ID maps IP addresses to usernames and AD groups, enabling identity-based security policies.

Without User-ID: "Allow 10.1.1.50 to access finance server"
  Problem: Who is 10.1.1.50? What if DHCP changes IP?

With User-ID: "Allow Finance-Department group to access finance server"
  Benefit: Works regardless of IP, laptop, or location

User-ID Source Methods

Active Directory (Most Common)

Monitors AD Event ID 4624 (login events). FW sees login, maps IP â†’ User.

Captive Portal

Unknown users redirected to login page. For BYOD, guests.

VPN Integration

VPN client provides identity at connection.

Syslog Parsing

Parse logs from 802.1X, NAC, wireless controllers.

13 Interview Questions & Answers

Q: What's the difference between a traditional firewall and an NGFW?

"So the fundamental difference comes down to what the firewall can see and understand. A traditional firewallâ€”think the classic Cisco ASA or older Check Pointâ€”operates at Layers 3 and 4. It sees IP addresses, ports, and protocols. If I write a rule that says 'allow TCP 443 outbound,' it'll permit anything on that port. The problem? Attackers figured this out years ago. They tunnel everything over 443 because they know it's usually open.

An NGFW flips this on its head. It operates at Layer 7â€”the application layerâ€”so it actually understands what application is running, not just which port it's using. Palo Alto calls this App-ID, Fortinet calls it Application Control. The firewall does deep packet inspection, looks at the payload, and identifies whether that traffic on port 443 is legitimate HTTPS, or if someone's trying to sneak BitTorrent or Tor through it.

But it goes beyond just application awareness. NGFWs integrate with Active Directory through User-ID, so instead of writing rules for IP addresses that change when someone moves desks, I write rules for user groups. 'Allow Marketing to access social media'â€”and it applies regardless of which device they're on.

Then you've got integrated security servicesâ€”IPS that's actually inline, not bolted on as a separate box. URL filtering that categorizes websites in real-time. Sandboxing that can detonate suspicious files before they reach endpoints. And critically, SSL decryptionâ€”because if you can't see inside encrypted traffic, you're blind to 90% of what's happening on your network.

The way I think about it: a traditional firewall is a bouncer checking IDs at the door. An NGFW is a bouncer who also knows what you're carrying, who you work for, what you're planning to do inside, and can call for backup if something looks suspicious."

Q: A user reports they can't access a website. Walk me through your troubleshooting process.

"Alright, so this is probably the most common request I deal with. First thing I do is resist the urge to immediately start clicking through firewall logsâ€”I need context first.

I ask the user: What's the exact URL? Not 'a website,' I need the specific address. What error do you see? A connection timeout is different from a block page. What time did this happen? Logs rotate, timestamps matter. Has this ever worked before? That tells me if it's a new application or a regression. And I get their IP addressâ€”either from them or from our asset inventory.

Now I go to the firewall. On a Palo Alto, I'd filter traffic logs: source IP equals the user's IP, destination contains the domain. I'm looking for whether the session was allowed, denied, or dropped. If it's denied, I check which rule matched and why.

The usual suspects? URL filtering blocked it as 'malware' or 'gambling' or whatever category. That's an easy fix if it's a false positiveâ€”I check the site reputation, and if it's legitimate, I add an exception. Sometimes App-ID is the culpritâ€”the firewall identified the application as something unexpected. I had a case where a legitimate business app was being classified as 'unknown-tcp' because it was custom software, so the rule allowing 'web-browsing' didn't match.

SSL decryption issues are sneaky. The site might use certificate pinning, so our decryption breaks the connection. The user sees a certificate error or the app just fails silently. I check if the domain is in our decryption exclusion listâ€”banking sites, healthcare, anything with compliance implications should already be excluded.

Once I identify the cause, I don't just fix it and move on. If it requires a rule change, that goes through change managementâ€”I document the business justification, the specific change, and the testing I'll do afterward. After implementing, I have the user test again while I watch the logs in real-time. Nothing worse than closing a ticket only to have them reply 'still broken.'

And if it turns out the block was validâ€”like they were trying to access something against policyâ€”I explain it professionally. 'This site is blocked under our acceptable use policy. If you believe this is a business need, please have your manager submit an exception request.' I'm not the policy police, but I do enforce what's been decided."

Q: Explain SSL/TLS decryption and the considerations around implementing it.

"SSL decryption is one of those capabilities that's absolutely essential but also comes with significant baggageâ€”technical, legal, and ethical.

Let me start with the why. Something like 95% of web traffic is now encrypted. That's great for privacy, but it's a nightmare for security. Without decryption, your firewall, your IPS, your DLPâ€”they're all blind. They see an encrypted blob going to an IP address. Could be someone checking Gmail, could be malware exfiltrating your customer database. You can't tell.

So how does it work? The firewall performs a man-in-the-middleâ€”and I know that sounds bad, but it's controlled and intentional. When a user connects to, say, example.com, the firewall intercepts the TLS handshake. It presents its own certificate to the user, signed by an internal CA. Then it establishes a separate encrypted connection to the real server. The firewall sits in the middle, decrypting traffic from the user, inspecting it, re-encrypting it, and sending it to the destination. The user's browser shows a valid certificate because their machine trusts our internal CAâ€”pushed via Group Policy.

Now the considerations. Performance is the first one. Decryption is CPU-intensive. Every session that gets decrypted adds load. On older firewalls, enabling decryption for all traffic could tank your throughput. Newer models have dedicated SSL processing chips, but you still need to size appropriately. We actually had to upgrade our firewall hardware when we expanded decryption because we were hitting 80% CPU during peak hours.

Privacy and legalâ€”this is where you need HR and Legal involved before you flip the switch. In some jurisdictions, intercepting encrypted traffic has legal implications. You need clear policies that employees acknowledge. 'Network traffic on corporate systems may be monitored and inspected.' And practically, you can't decrypt everything. Banking sitesâ€”never touch those, regulatory nightmare. Healthcare portalsâ€”HIPAA implications. Government sitesâ€”they often use certificate pinning anyway.

Technical exclusions are a whole category. Certificate pinning is when an app is hardcoded to expect a specific certificate, not just any valid one. If you try to decrypt, the app breaks. Mobile apps are notorious for this. Windows Update, Apple services, video conferencing toolsâ€”these typically need exclusions. I maintain a pretty extensive exclusion list, and it's something I review quarterly because apps change their infrastructure.

Certificate deployment is the operational challenge. Every client needs to trust your decryption CA. For domain-joined Windows machines, Group Policy handles it. For BYO devices, it's harderâ€”you might need MDM enrollment. And guest networks? We just don't decrypt guest trafficâ€”it's not worth the support headache.

The payoff is worth it though. Once we enabled decryption, we caught things we never knew were happening. Malware callbacks hidden in HTTPS. Users uploading sensitive files to personal cloud storage. Even legitimate security issues like outdated TLS versions that we couldn't see before. You can't protect what you can't see."

Q: How do you approach firewall rule cleanup and optimization?

"Rule cleanup is one of those unglamorous but critical tasks that separates mature security programs from chaotic ones. I've inherited firewalls with 500+ rules where nobody could tell you what half of them did. That's technical debt that creates real risk.

My approach starts with data gathering. I pull a rule usage reportâ€”both Palo Alto and FortiGate can show you hit counts over time. I'm specifically looking for rules with zero hits in the last 90 days. But here's the thingâ€”I don't just delete them. A rule might exist for a quarterly process that hasn't run yet. Or disaster recovery that thankfully hasn't been triggered.

So my process is disable first, delete later. I disable the rule and add a comment with the date and my initials: 'Disabled 2024-03-15 NK - Zero hits 90 days - Delete after 2024-04-15 if no issues.' Then I wait 30 days. If nobody screams, I delete it in the next maintenance window. If something breaks, re-enabling takes 30 seconds.

Beyond unused rules, I look for shadow rulesâ€”rules that can never match because a broader rule above them catches the traffic first. Classic example: Rule 10 allows 'any to any on port 443.' Rule 47 allows 'Sales group to CRM server on port 443.' Rule 47 will never match because Rule 10 already caught it. These are dangerous because someone thinks they removed access to the CRM by deleting Rule 47, but traffic still flows through Rule 10.

I also hunt for overly permissive rules. Anything with 'any' in source, destination, or service is a red flag. Sometimes they're legitimateâ€”DNS needs to be available to everyoneâ€”but often they're lazy rules that someone created during a late-night troubleshooting session and never tightened. 'Just allow any-any for now, we'll fix it later.' Later never comes.

Rule order optimization matters for performance. Firewalls evaluate rules top-to-bottom and stop at first match. If your most-hit rule is at position 200, every session evaluates 199 rules that don't match before hitting it. I move high-traffic rules toward the topâ€”within logical sections, of course. I'm not putting a server rule before my user rules just for performance.

Finally, I look at consolidation opportunities. Ten rules allowing different subnets to the same server can often become one rule using an address group. This isn't just aestheticsâ€”fewer rules means faster evaluation and easier auditing.

I do this cleanup quarterly, and I track metrics: total rule count over time, percentage of rules with hits in 90 days, number of 'any' rules. Management likes seeing those numbers trend in the right direction, and it keeps me honest about maintaining hygiene."

Q: Walk me through handling an emergency request to block a malicious IP.

"Emergency blocks are where you earn your paycheck. The SOC calls and says 'We're seeing active C2 communication to this IP, we need it blocked NOW.' Adrenaline kicks in, but you have to stay methodical.

First 60 secondsâ€”I'm acting, not debating. I log into the firewall, create an address object for the malicious IP with a clear name: 'BLOCK-IOC-20240315-C2' so it's obvious what it is later. I create a deny rule at the very top of the policyâ€”above everything elseâ€”source any, destination that IP, deny and log. On Palo Alto: commit. On FortiGate: save. That IP is now blocked across the enterprise.

Verificationâ€”I don't trust that it worked just because I clicked save. I check the traffic logs in real-time. If any internal host tries to reach that IP, I should see deny entries hitting my new rule. If the SOC can tell me a specific compromised host, I filter for that source and confirm blocks are happening.

Then I breathe and start the investigation. This is where the real work begins. I pull historical logsâ€”last 24 hours, last 7 daysâ€”for any traffic to or from that IP. Which internal hosts communicated with it? When did it start? How much data transferred? This tells me the scope of potential compromise. I feed this back to the SOC and the IR team.

Documentation happens in parallel. I open an incident ticket if one doesn't exist. I document exactly what I did, when I did it, and what I observed. Screenshots of the rule, log samples showing blocks. This matters for post-incident review and for legal if this turns into something bigger.

IOC expansionâ€”one IP is rarely the whole story. I check threat intel feeds for associated indicators. Same malware family often uses multiple C2 servers. Follow-upâ€”after the immediate fire is out, I review whether this was something our existing controls should have caught. Did our threat prevention miss it? Is there a signature we need to enable? The whole philosophy is contain fast, investigate thoroughly, improve continuously. That emergency block might stay in place for days while IR does their work, but eventually it either gets moved to our permanent threat blocklist or removed once we've confirmed the threat is neutralized."

Q: How do you balance security controls with business needs and agility?

"This is honestly one of the harder parts of the jobâ€”and where a lot of security people get it wrong. Neither serves the organization.

My philosophy is security should enable the business, not obstruct itâ€”but that doesn't mean saying yes to bad ideas. It means finding secure ways to accomplish business objectives.

Practically, I use a tiered change process. Standard changesâ€”things we do regularly with known risk profilesâ€”have pre-approved procedures. Opening a port for a well-understood application with proper documentation? I can do that same-day with just my review. It still gets logged and documented, but I'm not creating bureaucracy for routine work.

Non-standard changes get more scrutiny. Someone wants to allow direct RDP from the internet? That's a conversation. I ask: what's the business need? Usually it's 'I want to work from home.' Okay, we have a VPN for that. Or 'The vendor needs access.' We have a vendor access process for that. I'm not just saying noâ€”I'm redirecting to secure alternatives that accomplish the same goal.

When business really pushes backâ€”'the CEO needs this, it's urgent'â€”I don't just cave. I document the risk clearly. 'This rule allows inbound RDP which exposes us to brute force attacks, BlueKeep exploitation, and credential theft. If you accept this risk, please acknowledge in writing.' Suddenly people find the VPN isn't so inconvenient after all. Risk acceptance has a way of clarifying priorities.

I also push back on scope creep. Someone asks to allow traffic to 'the vendor's servers.' Which servers? What ports? What application? I'm not creating 'any any' rules because someone couldn't be bothered to get specifics. Least privilege isn't negotiableâ€”but I'll work with them to define exactly what they need.

Emergency procedures exist for genuine emergencies. If production is down and we need to open something immediately, I'll do itâ€”but it comes with a mandatory review within 48 hours to either properly document and secure it, or remove it. No 'temporary' rules lasting three years.

Finally, I maintain relationships. I have coffee with the networking team, I chat with developers, I understand what projects are coming. When I know context, I can proactively identify security requirements instead of being surprised by last-minute requests. 'Hey, I heard you're deploying a new CRM.
