Collect evidence in this order - most volatile first!

  1. 1. Memory (RAM) - Disappears on power off
  2. 2. Network connections - Active sessions
  3. 3. Running processes - Current state
  4. 4. Disk - Persists but can be modified
  5. 5. Logs - May rotate/overwrite
  6. 6. Physical evidence - Most stable

Interview Q&A

Q: Walk me through how you would respond to a ransomware incident.

"Following PICERL: First, Identification - confirm it's ransomware, determine variant, identify affected systems. Containment - immediately isolate infected machines from network, disable affected accounts, block C2 domains. Eradication - remove ransomware binaries, scan for persistence mechanisms. Recovery - restore from known-good backups (never pay ransom as first option), rebuild if needed. Lessons Learned - how did they get in? Improve defenses. Throughout: document everything, preserve evidence, communicate with stakeholders."

Q: What's the difference between short-term and long-term containment?

"Short-term is immediate triage - isolate the system, block the IP, disable the account. Goal is to stop the bleeding NOW. Long-term is sustainable remediation - patch the vulnerability, implement proper segmentation, rebuild with hardened images. Short-term is a band-aid; long-term is proper surgery. You need both - short-term buys you time for long-term."

Q: Why is the order of volatility important in forensics?

"Evidence disappears at different rates. RAM is gone instantly on power off - collect it first. Network connections and processes are next. Disk is more stable but can be modified. Logs can rotate. If you start with disk while RAM has critical evidence, you lose it. Always collect most volatile first. Mnemonic: 'Memory, Network, Processes, Disk, Logs, Physical' - most to least volatile."
