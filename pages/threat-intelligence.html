Threat Hunting

ğŸ¯ What is Threat Hunting?

Proactively searching for threats that evade existing detection. Unlike reactive alerting, hunting assumes the adversary is already in your environment.

1

Hypothesis-Driven

"If APT28 targeted us, they would use X technique"

2

IOC-Based

"Let me search for these known malicious indicators"

3

Anomaly-Based

"What's unusual or statistically rare in our data?"

ğŸ“‹ Threat Hunt Process

1

Form Hypothesis

Based on TI, ATT&CK, or anomalies. "Adversaries may be using PowerShell for lateral movement."

2

Collect Data

Identify relevant data sources. Query SIEM, EDR, network logs.

3

Analyze

Look for patterns, anomalies, known-bad indicators. Pivot and drill down.

4

Document Findings

Record what you found - threats, gaps, or nothing (still valuable).

5

Create Detection

Turn successful hunts into automated detection rules for future alerts.

KQL Threat Hunting Queries

Hunt: PowerShell Encoded Commands (T1059.001)

DeviceProcessEvents
| where Timestamp > ago(7d)
| where FileName =~ "powershell.exe"
| where ProcessCommandLine has_any ("-enc", "-encodedcommand", "-e ", "FromBase64")
| project Timestamp, DeviceName, AccountName, ProcessCommandLine
| order by Timestamp desc

Hunt: LSASS Memory Access (T1003.001 - Mimikatz)

DeviceProcessEvents
| where Timestamp > ago(7d)
| where FileName !in~ ("lsass.exe", "csrss.exe", "svchost.exe")
| where ProcessCommandLine has_any ("lsass", "sekurlsa", "logonpasswords")
   or InitiatingProcessFileName =~ "rundll32.exe" and FileName =~ "lsass.exe"
| project Timestamp, DeviceName, FileName, ProcessCommandLine, AccountName

Hunt: Suspicious Scheduled Tasks (T1053.005)

DeviceProcessEvents
| where Timestamp > ago(7d)
| where FileName =~ "schtasks.exe"
| where ProcessCommandLine has "/create"
| where ProcessCommandLine has_any ("powershell", "cmd", "wscript", "cscript", "mshta", "rundll32")
| project Timestamp, DeviceName, AccountName, ProcessCommandLine

Hunt: Remote Service Creation (T1021.002 - PSExec-like)

DeviceProcessEvents
| where Timestamp > ago(7d)
| where InitiatingProcessFileName =~ "services.exe"
| where FileName in~ ("cmd.exe", "powershell.exe")
| project Timestamp, DeviceName, AccountName, FileName, ProcessCommandLine

Hunt: IOC Search (Multiple Indicators)

let malicious_ips = dynamic(["1.2.3.4", "5.6.7.8"]);
let malicious_domains = dynamic(["evil.com", "malware.net"]);
let malicious_hashes = dynamic(["abc123...", "def456..."]);
union DeviceNetworkEvents, DeviceFileEvents
| where Timestamp > ago(30d)
| where RemoteIP in (malicious_ips)
   or RemoteUrl has_any (malicious_domains)
   or SHA256 in (malicious_hashes)
| project Timestamp, DeviceName, ActionType, RemoteIP, RemoteUrl, SHA256

Interview Questions & Answers

Q: Explain MITRE ATT&CK and how you use it.

"MITRE ATT&CK is a knowledge base of adversary tactics, techniques, and procedures based on real-world observations. It organizes attacker behavior into 14 tactics (the 'why' - like Initial Access, Execution, Persistence) and hundreds of techniques (the 'how').

I use ATT&CK in several ways:

Detection Engineering: When writing detection rules, I map them to ATT&CK techniques. This helps identify coverage gaps - if we have no detections for T1003 (Credential Dumping), we know we need to add some.

Threat Hunting: I use ATT&CK to form hypotheses. 'If APT29 targeted us, they commonly use T1566 (Phishing) and T1059 (PowerShell) - let me hunt for those behaviors.'

Incident Response: During investigations, I map observed behaviors to ATT&CK to understand the attack lifecycle and predict what the adversary might do next."

Q: What's the difference between IOCs and TTPs? Which is more valuable?

"IOCs (Indicators of Compromise) are specific artifacts like IP addresses, domains, and file hashes. TTPs (Tactics, Techniques, and Procedures) describe adversary behavior - how they operate.

TTPs are more valuable because they're harder for attackers to change. The Pyramid of Pain illustrates this - hashes are at the bottom (trivial to change by recompiling), while TTPs are at the top (require significant effort to change behavior).

For example, if I detect malware by its hash, the attacker just recompiles and gets a new hash. But if I detect the behavior - like 'PowerShell downloading and executing code from the internet' - that's harder to avoid because it's how the attack works.

That said, IOCs still have value for immediate blocking and historical analysis. The best approach combines both - IOCs for quick wins, TTPs for durable detection."

Q: How would you conduct a threat hunt?

"I follow a structured hypothesis-driven approach:

1. Form Hypothesis: Based on threat intelligence or ATT&CK. For example, 'Ransomware groups often disable Windows Defender before deploying - let me look for that behavior (T1562.001).'

2. Identify Data Sources: What logs do I need? For this hunt, I'd need EDR process logs, specifically looking for commands that modify Defender settings.

3. Build Query: Create a KQL/SPL query to find the behavior - processes executing 'Set-MpPreference -DisableRealtimeMonitoring' or similar.

4. Analyze Results: Review findings, eliminate false positives (IT admins during patching), investigate suspicious activity.

5. Document & Detect: Even if I find nothing, I document the hunt. If I find a real technique, I turn the query into an automated detection rule."

Q: How do you integrate threat intelligence into your security operations?

"I integrate TI at multiple levels:

Automated IOC Ingestion: Feed TI platforms (like Microsoft Defender TI or MISP) directly into SIEM. Automatic correlation against network and endpoint logs generates alerts when IOCs are seen.

Alert Enrichment: During triage, I automatically enrich IOCs with TI context - is this IP known malicious? What's the VirusTotal score? This speeds up analysis.

Detection Rule Development: When new threat reports come out (like a new ransomware variant), I extract TTPs and create detection rules before IOCs are even published.

Proactive Hunting: Use TI on threat actors targeting our industry to form hunt hypotheses. If a report says APT group X is targeting manufacturing with specific techniques, I hunt for those in our environment."
