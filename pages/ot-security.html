12 Interview Questions & Answers

Q1: How is OT security different from IT security?

"This is honestly one of the first things I had to unlearn when I started looking into OT security, because the instincts you develop in IT don't always translate.

In IT, we live by the CIA triadâ€”Confidentiality, Integrity, Availability. Protect the data first, make sure it's accurate, then worry about uptime. In OT, it's completely inverted. Actually, there's an even higher priority than availability: Safety. A misconfigured PLC in a chemical plant could cause an explosion. A compromised controller in a food processing facility could contaminate product. We're not just talking about data breachesâ€”we're talking about physical harm to people.

So the priority stack becomes Safety, Availability, Integrity, then Confidentiality. And this changes everything about how you approach security.

Patchingâ€”in IT, I'm used to monthly patch cycles, Patch Tuesday, automatic updates. In OT? Some systems get patched annually, during planned maintenance shutdowns. And even then, you can't just apply the latest Windows update to an HMI running a 15-year-old SCADA application. You have to test exhaustively, get vendor approval, have rollback plans ready. I've read about plants that still run Windows XPâ€”not because they don't know it's end-of-life, but because the control software that runs their $10 million production line only works on XP, and the vendor went out of business.

Scanningâ€”in IT, I'd run Nessus or Qualys to find vulnerabilities. In OT, aggressive network scanning can actually crash PLCs. These controllers weren't designed to handle a barrage of unexpected packets. So we use passive monitoringâ€”tap into a SPAN port, watch traffic, never inject anything. You're observing, not probing.

Downtimeâ€”in IT, I can take a server down for maintenance at 2 AM on Sunday. In OT, some processes run 24/7 for months between scheduled shutdowns. A bottling line running continuouslyâ€”if you stop it mid-batch, you might ruin thousands of dollars of product. The business impact of unplanned downtime is measured in tens of thousands of dollars per hour.

Lifecycleâ€”IT assets last 3-5 years before refresh. OT equipment runs for 15, 20, sometimes 25+ years. That PLC installed in 2005 is still controlling a critical process, and it was designed before cybersecurity was a consideration. You can't just swap it outâ€”it's integrated into the physical process.

The mindset shift is moving from 'secure it properly' to 'secure it appropriately without breaking production.' Compensating controls become your best friend. Can't patch? Segment. Can't install an agent? Monitor passively. Can't authenticate? Restrict network access. It's security with constraints that IT rarely faces."

Q2: Explain the Purdue Model and why it matters for OT security.

"The Purdue Modelâ€”or more formally, the Purdue Enterprise Reference Architectureâ€”is basically the blueprint for how industrial networks should be structured. It's been around since the 90s, but it's still the foundation for modern OT security standards like IEC 62443.

Think of it as a layered cake with clear boundaries. Let me walk through it from bottom to top, because that's how the physical process flows.

Level 0 is the physical process itselfâ€”sensors measuring temperature, pressure, flow rates. Motors, valves, pumps, conveyors. This is actual machinery that moves atoms, not bits. These devices have no concept of security; they respond to electrical signals from the controllers above them.

Level 1 is where the PLCs and RTUs liveâ€”Programmable Logic Controllers and Remote Terminal Units. These are the brains that execute control logic. 'If temperature exceeds 40 degrees, open the cooling valve.' They run deterministic code in real-time loops, often thousands of times per second. Safety Instrumented Systems also live hereâ€”independent safety layers that can shut down a process if something dangerous is detected.

Level 2 is area supervisory controlâ€”the HMIs, or Human-Machine Interfaces. This is where operators interact with the process. Touch screens showing tank levels, pump status, alarm conditions. Each production areaâ€”say, bottling, packaging, palletizingâ€”has its own HMIs. Operators make adjustments, acknowledge alarms, start and stop sequences from here.

Level 3 is site operationsâ€”SCADA servers, historians, engineering workstations. This is where you see the whole plant, not just one area. Process engineers analyze trends from the historian data. The SCADA system aggregates data from all the PLCs and presents a unified view.

Now here's where it gets critical for security: between Level 3 and Level 4 sits the Industrial DMZ. This is the security boundary between OT and IT. Jump hosts for remote access go here. OT monitoring platforms like Claroty or Nozomi. Data transfer servers that move historian data to business systems. Patch servers that stage updates before they go to OT. The DMZ is where you broker all interaction between the worlds.

Level 4 is site business planningâ€”production scheduling, inventory management, reporting dashboards. This is technically IT, but it needs data from OT to function.

Level 5 is the enterprise networkâ€”ERP, email, cloud services, corporate users. Standard IT security applies here.

The golden rule is: no direct connections between Level 4-5 and Level 0-3. Everything goes through the DMZ. If an attacker compromises a corporate workstation, they can't pivot directly into PLCs. They'd have to get through the IT firewall, land in the DMZ, compromise a DMZ service, get through the OT firewall, and then reach operational assets. Each boundary is a chance to detect and stop them.

This is why the Purdue Model mattersâ€”it provides defense in depth architecturally. It's not just a nice diagram; it's the structural basis for keeping production safe from cyber threats that enter through the corporate network. IEC 62443 formalizes this with the concept of zones and conduits, but Purdue is where it all started."

Q3: How would you handle vulnerability management in an OT environment?

"Vulnerability management in OT is an exercise in creative problem-solving, because the playbook from IT just doesn't work.

Discovery is the first challenge. In IT, I'd deploy Nessus, run authenticated scans, get a comprehensive vulnerability report in a few hours. In OT? Active scanning is dangerous. I've read reports of PLCs crashing from Nmap scansâ€”these devices weren't designed to handle unexpected network traffic. Some will reset, some will freeze, some will fail into an unsafe state.

So we use passive discovery. Deploy a sensor on a SPAN port or network TAP, let it listen to traffic for a few weeks. Tools like Claroty, Nozomi, or Dragos can identify OT assets by analyzing the protocols they speak. 'Oh, there's Modbus traffic to this IPâ€”that's probably a PLC. The firmware banner says it's a Rockwell 1756-L73 running version 31.011.' You build your asset inventory without touching anything.

Vulnerability correlation comes next. Once I know what assets I have, I cross-reference against ICS-CERT advisories, vendor security bulletins, and CVE databases. 'We have 15 Siemens S7-1500 PLCs running firmware 2.8.3â€”are there any known vulnerabilities?' The OT monitoring platforms automate a lot of this, highlighting assets with known CVEs.

Here's where it gets interesting: risk assessment in OT is different. A vulnerability might have a CVSS score of 9.8â€”critical in IT terms. But in OT, I have to ask additional questions:

â€¢ Is this asset network-accessible? A PLC on an isolated cell network with no route to IT is harder to exploit than one on a converged network.
â€¢ Is there a known exploit? Theoretical vulnerabilities are lower priority than ones with Metasploit modules available.
â€¢ What's the safety impact? A vulnerability in an HMI displaying production metrics is different from one in a Safety Instrumented System.
â€¢ Can we compensate? Network segmentation might reduce a critical to a low if the attack path is blocked.

Remediation is where we really diverge from IT. Patching a PLC isn't like patching Windows. First, you need the vendor's blessingâ€”applying an untested firmware update could break the control logic. Then you need a maintenance window, which might be months away. You need a tested rollback plan because if the update fails mid-production, you need to recover fast.

More often than not, the answer is compensating controls. Can't patch the PLC? Put a firewall rule in front of it that only allows traffic from its designated HMI. Can't update the Windows XP HMI?
