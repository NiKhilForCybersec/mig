Social Engineering Tactics

üß† SE Principles: "RUSSIA"

Reciprocity - Give to get

Urgency - Act now!

Social proof - Others did it

Scarcity - Limited time/offer

Intimidation - Authority/fear

Authority - I'm from IT/CEO

Pretexting

Creating false scenario to obtain info. "I'm from IT, need your password to fix your account."

Baiting

Leaving infected USB drives. "Free music" downloads with malware.

Tailgating/Piggybacking

Following authorized person through secure door without badging in.

Quid Pro Quo

Offering something in exchange. "Free tech support" that installs malware.

Interview Q&A

Q: How would you design a security awareness program?

"Start with a baseline phishing test to measure current state. Then implement role-based training - executives need BEC awareness, finance needs wire fraud training, developers need secure coding. Use multiple delivery methods - video modules, simulations, newsletters. Run regular simulations (monthly) with increasing difficulty. Measure and report metrics to leadership - click rates, report rates, training completion. Create positive culture - reward reporters, don't punish clickers (train instead)."

Q: What are the signs of a phishing email?

"Use the SUSMAIL checklist: Sender address looks off (slightly misspelled domains), Urgency or threats ('act now or lose access'), Spelling/grammar errors, Mismatched links (hover shows different URL), Attachments unexpected (especially .exe, .zip), Information requests (credentials, payment), Looks too good (you won!) or too scary (legal action). When in doubt, verify through a separate channel - call the person directly using a known number."

Q: How do you handle an employee who repeatedly falls for phishing simulations?

"Progressive approach: First failure gets immediate training with explanation. Second failure gets one-on-one coaching. Third failure involves manager notification and required additional training. Throughout, keep it educational not punitive - we want people to report, not hide mistakes. Also investigate why they're susceptible - is it time pressure, lack of technical knowledge, or the simulation was too sophisticated? Consider reducing their access to sensitive data if the risk is too high."
